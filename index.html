Curation Notes

Genre Diversity
The couple's 10 required songs already span classic rock, hip-hop, R&B, funk, country, and neo-soul. Every additional song reinforces one of these pillars so the playlist feels deliberate rather than scattered.
Generational Range
With guests spanning multiple generations, the playlist covers five decades. Older guests get Stevie Wonder, Bon Jovi, and Etta James; the couple and their peers have Drake, Dua Lipa, and Lizzo. Cross-generational crowd-pleasers like September and Uptown Funk bridge every age group.
Blending Songs (8)
These 8 songs sit intentionally between genres. Old Town Road merges country and hip-hop in a single track. Valerie and Come and Get Your Love let a DJ pivot from soul into rock or funk without a jarring jump. Smooth and Moves Like Jagger serve as rock entries that feel accessible to R&B-leaning guests.
Genre-Extreme Songs (12)
These 12 push each genre toward its harder, rawer, or more traditional edge. HUMBLE., Sicko Mode, and Power bring real hip-hop intensity. Jolene, Friends in Low Places, and The House That Built Me are classic country without pop polish. Back in Black, Thunderstruck, Welcome to the Jungle, and Pour Some Sugar on Me are full-throttle rock.
Dance Floor Energy Arc
A reception needs pacing: a warm opening (Romantic, Country, Neo-Soul), a ramp-up (90s R&B, Hip-Hop), a peak floor (Funk/Disco, Dance Pop), and a wind-down close. The 100-song depth gives the DJ flexibility to read the room without repeating songs.
Bride's Dance Background
Because the bride competed in dance team, selections favor strong rhythmic foundations — syncopated bass lines and groove-heavy arrangements. The Funk/Disco and 90s R&B blocks in particular reward a dancer who knows how to move to a pocket groove.

Phase Notes

Phase 1 — Arrival & Cocktail
Warm and background-friendly regardless of genre. The goal is music that welcomes guests and sets a celebratory mood without demanding attention. Songs from any era are welcome here as long as they work at low volume while people arrive, find seats, and start conversations.
Phase 2 — Dinner
Mid-tempo and recognizable — songs people can sing along to without shouting across the table. Genre doesn't matter; energy does. A 70s soul classic, a 2000s ballad, and a current pop track all belong here if they're familiar and sit at the right tempo.
Phase 3 — Warm-Up
Energy building, floor-adjacent. People are finishing dinner and moving toward the bar — the music should make them feel like dancing is coming without fully committing yet. Any genre works here: 90s R&B, 2000s hip-hop, classic funk, dance pop. The bar is "groovy but not yet a banger."
Phase 4 — Dance Floor Opens
The floor is open and people are moving. This phase is mixed-genre by design — the DJ draws from funk, hip-hop, pop, and R&B freely to keep the floor inclusive. Songs were chosen because they make people stand up, not because of their era or genre label.
Phase 5 — Peak Energy
Maximum intensity for the top of the night. Again genre-agnostic: harder hip-hop, full-throttle rock, high-BPM dance pop all share this phase because they serve the same purpose — keeping a packed floor at full energy. The couple and their generation own this block.
Phase 6 — Grand Finale
Cross-generational, room-wide sing-alongs that feel like a send-off. The energy is still high but shifts from intensity to communal joy. Every song in this block should have the whole room — all ages — singing together as the night closes.
